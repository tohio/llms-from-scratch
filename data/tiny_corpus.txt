The lighthouse had been dark for three years when Maria first arrived on the island.
She came with two suitcases, a box of books, and no intention of leaving. The ferry
captain warned her about the winters. She thanked him and walked up the hill anyway.

The cottage beside the lighthouse was smaller than the photographs suggested. One
room served as kitchen, living space, and study. A narrow staircase led to a bedroom
barely wide enough for a bed and a wooden chest. The lighthouse itself was locked.
She had the key but did not use it that first night.

In the morning she walked the perimeter of the island. It took forty minutes at a
slow pace. The cliffs on the northern side dropped sharply into cold green water. On
the southern shore a small beach of dark sand collected driftwood and the occasional
fishing float in faded orange and blue. She picked up a piece of driftwood and
carried it back to the cottage without knowing why.

Her work was translation. German academic texts into English, slow and precise work
that suited her temperament. She set up her desk facing the window that looked out
toward the mainland, invisible on most days behind fog and distance. The silence was
not empty. It had texture. Wind against glass. The low percussion of waves. Birds
she could not yet name.

On the fourth day she climbed the lighthouse.

The stairs were iron, painted grey, worn smooth in the centre of each step by decades
of keepers ascending and descending with the regularity of tides. The light mechanism
at the top was dusty but intact. She cleaned the glass with a cloth she found hanging
on a hook as if left for exactly that purpose. Through the clean glass the sea looked
close enough to touch.

She did not turn the light on. That was not her job. But she sat beside it until
the sun went down and the stars came out over the water, and she felt for the first
time in a long while that she was exactly where she was supposed to be.

Winter came early that year. The ferry stopped running in November and did not resume
until March. She had provisioned well. Flour, rice, dried beans, tinned fish, coffee,
and more books than she could read in a single winter though she tried. The generator
ran four hours each evening. She learned to work by candlelight and found she
preferred it.

She translated a dense philosophical text about the nature of memory. She read it
slowly, turning sentences over like stones, looking at what lived underneath. The
author argued that memory was not storage but reconstruction, that every act of
remembering was also an act of invention. She thought about this while watching the
sea. She thought the sea probably agreed.

In February a storm lasted eleven days. The cottage shook but held. She burned
driftwood in the small iron stove and listened to the lighthouse groan above her.
On the eighth day of the storm she climbed the tower again and watched waves strike
the rocks below with a force that seemed impossible and impersonal and magnificent.
She was not afraid. She was paying attention.

The ferry returned on a Tuesday in late March carrying mail, supplies, and a young
man named David who had been hired to inspect the lighthouse mechanism. He was
surprised to find her there. Most people were, she explained. He stayed three days,
ate dinner with her twice, and left on the Friday ferry. He said the mechanism was
in good order. She said she was glad to hear it.

Spring arrived incrementally. First a loosening of the cold. Then light staying
longer each evening. Then the birds she had spent the winter learning to name
returning to the cliffs in numbers that seemed celebratory. She finished the
translation in April and sent it to her editor on the mainland. Her editor called
it her best work. She did not tell him about the lighthouse or the storm or the
eleven days in February. Some contexts cannot be translated.

She ordered supplies for another year.

The lighthouse had been dark for four years when the harbour authority wrote to ask
if she had noticed any issues with the mechanism. She wrote back to say the mechanism
was in good order. She did not mention that she climbed the tower every evening now
and sat beside the cold light watching the sea move in the dark below her, patient
and enormous and indifferent to whether anyone was watching.

She was watching. That felt like enough.
